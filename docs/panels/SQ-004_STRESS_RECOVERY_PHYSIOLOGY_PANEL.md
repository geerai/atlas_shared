# Panel Dossier: SQ-004

## Question

What physiological markers (cortisol, heart rate, skin conductance) show recovery after exposure to natural versus urban environments?

## Topic / Subtopic

Stress Recovery Theory / Physiological measures

## In-Criteria (Central Cases)

1. The study exposes human participants to natural versus urban/built environments and measures at least one physiological stress biomarker: salivary cortisol, serum cortisol, salivary alpha-amylase, heart rate (HR), heart rate variability (HRV), skin conductance level (SCL), blood pressure (systolic/diastolic), or respiratory rate.
2. The study uses a within-subjects or between-subjects design where the environmental condition is the independent variable or primary contrast, and physiological recovery or change is the dependent variable.
3. The study reports time-series or pre-post physiological measurements that allow assessment of whether recovery occurred (not just a single snapshot).
4. Epidemiological or large-cohort studies linking green space exposure to chronic physiological outcomes (e.g., lower resting cortisol, lower cardiovascular risk) as population-level evidence of the stress recovery pathway.

## Out-Criteria (Clear Rejections)

1. Papers measuring only self-reported stress, mood, or affect with no physiological biomarker — these provide psychological evidence for SRT but do not address SQ-004's specific physiological focus.
2. Papers studying physiological responses to indoor exercise, meditation, or relaxation techniques that do not involve environmental manipulation — stress recovery from exercise is a different mechanism.
3. Papers about occupational stress or workplace burnout that measure cortisol but do not manipulate or assess environmental exposure — the environment must be the independent variable, not just the backdrop.
4. Papers about environmental toxicology (pollution, noise, chemical exposure) where the physiological outcome is harm rather than recovery — SQ-004 is about restorative exposure, not harmful exposure.
5. Animal studies of stress physiology in natural settings — SQ-004 specifies human participants.

## Hard Cases (Borderline Decisions)

1. **Forest bathing studies with cultural specificity**: Shinrin-yoku studies from Japan measuring cortisol, NK cell activity, and blood pressure. These are directly relevant but raise the question of whether findings transfer to non-Japanese populations (see SQ-009). *Tend toward: include as direct evidence for SQ-004; flag the population transfer question as a cross-reference to SQ-009.*

2. **Nature exposure + cortisol but the cortisol measure is a single morning sample**: A single cortisol measurement does not capture diurnal slope or recovery dynamics. *Tend toward: include but flag as methodologically limited — Hunter's "nature pill" research shows that diurnal slope measures are needed for robust inference.*

3. **HRV studies in parks vs. streets**: Heart rate variability measured during walking in green vs. urban settings. HRV is a valid autonomic measure, but the walking confound (exercise affects HRV) complicates interpretation. *Tend toward: include if the study controls for walking speed/intensity; edge case if not.*

4. **Hospital nature-view studies measuring clinical recovery outcomes**: Ulrich (1984) and successors measuring length of stay, analgesic use, and complication rates as proxies for physiological recovery. These are clinical outcomes, not direct physiological biomarkers. *Tend toward: include as indirect evidence — clinical recovery implies physiological recovery — but note that no biomarker was directly measured.*

5. **Studies using wearable biosensors in free-living conditions**: Ecological momentary assessment (EMA) studies tracking physiological responses as people move through green and urban environments during daily life. Ecologically valid but noisy. *Tend toward: include as complementary evidence alongside lab-controlled studies.*

## False Friends

- **"Physiological ecology"** — a field of ecology studying organism-environment interactions, not human stress physiology
- **"Environmental stress"** — often refers to pollution, heat waves, or climate change impacts, not nature-based recovery
- **"Cortisol" without environmental manipulation** — occupational health, clinical endocrinology
- **"Heart rate recovery"** — often refers to post-exercise cardiovascular recovery, not post-stress environmental recovery
- **"Nature-based solutions"** — often refers to ecosystem services (flood management, carbon capture), not human physiology

## Evidence Markers

- Named biomarkers: salivary cortisol, serum cortisol, alpha-amylase, heart rate, heart rate variability (HRV), skin conductance (SCL/SCR), blood pressure, respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA), natural killer (NK) cell activity
- Pre-post design with time-stamped physiological measurements
- Environmental conditions described as "nature," "forest," "green space," "park" vs. "urban," "city," "built," "indoor"
- References to Ulrich (1983, 1991, 1993), Miyazaki, Li et al. (NK cells), or Hunter et al. (nature pill)

## Escalation Triggers

1. **Papers with "stress" and "nature" that measure only self-reported stress.** Keyword filters will flag these, but SQ-004 specifically asks for physiological markers. The adjudicator must verify that a biomarker was actually measured, not just self-reported stress on a Likert scale.

2. **Papers measuring immune markers (NK cells, immunoglobulins) after forest exposure.** These are physiological and clearly related to SRT, but they are immune markers, not the classic "cortisol, heart rate, skin conductance" trio named in the question. The adjudicator must decide whether immune function counts as a "physiological marker" under the question's intent. *Recommended: yes — the question's parenthetical list is illustrative, not exhaustive.*

3. **Multi-measure studies where the physiological results are mixed.** A study showing cortisol decrease but no HRV change after nature exposure. Both the positive and null results are informative. The adjudicator should accept the paper and note the mixed findings.

## Unresolved Ambiguities

- Should EEG, fMRI, or other neuroimaging measures count as "physiological markers" for SQ-004? They are physiological (brain activity), but the question's exemplars (cortisol, heart rate, skin conductance) are all peripheral physiological markers. Neural measures may be better mapped to a separate neuroarchitecture question.
- Should chronic physiological outcomes (reduced hypertension risk from living near green space) count? These are physiological but epidemiological rather than experimental.

## LLM Drafting Note

This constitution was drafted with LLM panel reasoning. SQ-004 has the broadest evidence base of the first 10 questions — the forest bathing literature alone provides dozens of studies. The main risk is over-inclusion. The constitution prioritizes studies that actually measure physiological time-course recovery rather than single-snapshot associations. Pruessner's inclusion on the panel ensures methodological rigor around cortisol measurement (a notoriously variable biomarker).
