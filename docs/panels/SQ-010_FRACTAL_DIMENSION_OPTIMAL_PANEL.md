# Panel Dossier: SQ-010

## Question

What is the evidence for a Goldilocks optimal fractal dimension (approximately D=1.3–1.5) in visual patterns that reduces psychophysiological stress?

## Topic / Subtopic

Fractal Patterns / Optimal range

## Status

`llm_panel_drafted`

## In-Criteria (Central Cases)

1. The study tests whether visual patterns with fractal dimension D ≈ 1.3–1.5 produce lower stress, greater aesthetic preference, or more relaxed physiological states than patterns with higher or lower fractal dimensions.
2. The study systematically varies fractal dimension as the independent variable and measures a psychophysiological or stress-related outcome (EEG alpha power, skin conductance, cortisol, self-reported stress, heart rate variability).
3. The study tests the "fractal fluency" hypothesis — that the human visual system is tuned to process mid-range fractals more efficiently, and this processing ease produces positive affective and physiological responses.
4. The study measures physiological or perceptual responses to natural fractal patterns (tree branching, coastlines, cloud formations) and relates them to computed fractal dimension of the stimuli.

## Out-Criteria (Clear Rejections)

1. Papers about fractal analysis as a mathematical technique without any human psychological or physiological response measurement — pure fractal geometry, signal processing, or computer graphics.
2. Papers about fractal patterns in medical imaging (fractal dimension of tumors, retinal vessels, bone density) — these use fractal analysis as a diagnostic tool, not as a stimulus for human response.
3. Papers about visual complexity or aesthetics that do not measure or compute fractal dimension — general complexity-preference studies belong to SQ-022.
4. Papers about fractals in music, soundscapes, or temporal patterns — the question specifies *visual* patterns.

## Hard Cases (Borderline Decisions)

1. **1/f noise studies**: Research on 1/f (pink noise) spatial frequency spectra in natural scenes. The 1/f spectrum is closely related to fractal dimension but is not identical. *Tend toward: include if the paper connects 1/f spectral properties to the D=1.3–1.5 range or to stress/preference outcomes.*

2. **Pollock painting studies**: Taylor's foundational work analyzed the fractal dimension of Jackson Pollock's drip paintings. These are art-perception studies, not environmental psychology. *Tend toward: include — they provide the empirical basis for the D=1.3–1.5 claim and inform the environmental application.*

3. **Landscape preference studies with implicit fractal content**: Studies showing preference for nature scenes that happen to have mid-range fractal features (e.g., moderately branching trees) but do not measure fractal dimension explicitly. *Tend toward: edge case — the fractal content is inferred, not measured. Flag for potential retrospective fractal analysis.*

4. **Architectural pattern studies**: Research on ornamental patterns, facades, or textures in buildings that invokes fractal concepts. These apply fractal theory to the built environment but may not test the D=1.3–1.5 prediction specifically. *Tend toward: include if fractal dimension is computed and stress/preference is measured; edge case if fractal language is used loosely.*

5. **Studies finding different optimal ranges**: A study that finds the optimal fractal dimension for preference is D=1.2 or D=1.6 rather than D=1.3–1.5. This contradicts the specific prediction but is highly relevant. *Tend toward: definitely include — counter-evidence is epistemically essential.*

## False Friends

- **"Fractal" in medical imaging** — computational diagnostic tool, not human stimulus
- **"Self-similar" in network science** — scale-free networks, not visual patterns
- **"Dimension" in psychology** — personality dimensions, cognitive dimensions, not geometric dimension
- **"Pattern complexity"** — may refer to task complexity or organizational patterns, not visual fractal patterns
- **"Stress fracture"** — orthopedic, not psychophysiological

## Evidence Markers

- Computed fractal dimension (D, D_f, D₂) of visual stimuli
- Named methods: box-counting method, power spectral analysis, lacunarity analysis
- Psychophysiological measures: EEG (especially alpha/theta power), skin conductance, cortisol, preference ratings
- References to Taylor et al. (1999, 2006, 2011), Spehar et al. (2003, 2015), Hägerhäll et al. (2004, 2015)
- Phrases: "fractal fluency," "mid-range fractals," "optimal complexity," "Goldilocks range"

## Escalation Triggers

1. **Papers measuring visual complexity or preference with natural scenes but not computing fractal dimension.** The scenes may be fractal but the paper does not measure D. The adjudicator must assess whether the stimuli are likely in the D=1.3–1.5 range based on the scene descriptions (e.g., natural landscapes typically are).

2. **Papers from the "edge-orientation entropy" (EOE) tradition** (Redies, Brachmann) that propose an alternative to fractal dimension as the predictor of aesthetic preference. These are directly relevant as competing accounts but will not contain the word "fractal." The adjudicator should accept them as counter-evidence.

3. **Papers testing the effect of architectural ornamentation on stress**, where the ornamentation is fractal-like (e.g., Islamic geometric patterns, Gothic tracery) but the researcher does not use fractal terminology. The adjudicator should assess whether the visual features are plausibly in the relevant fractal range.

## Unresolved Ambiguities

- Is D=1.3–1.5 actually the optimal range, or is the range stimulus-dependent (different for natural scenes, abstract patterns, architectural elements)? The literature is not fully consistent.
- Is the "Goldilocks" effect about fractal dimension specifically, or about intermediate visual complexity more generally? If the latter, then fractal dimension is a proxy for complexity, and the question should be broadened to SQ-022 territory.

## LLM Drafting Note

This constitution was drafted with LLM panel reasoning. SQ-010 is distinctive because the evidence base is concentrated in a relatively small research group (Taylor, Spehar, Hägerhäll). The constitution must be careful not to merely describe one laboratory's program — it needs to include independent replications, failures to replicate, and alternative accounts. The inclusion of Spehar (who has published both supportive and critical work) and Pentland (computational complexity, not fractals specifically) provides intellectual balance.
