# Panel Dossier: SQ-005

## Question

What evidence directly tests the hypothesis that safety signalling — rather than mere pleasantness — explains why savanna-like landscapes reduce stress?

## Topic / Subtopic

Stress Recovery Theory / Safety signalling

## In-Criteria (Central Cases)

1. The study explicitly tests whether savanna-like landscape features (open grassland, scattered trees, prospect-refuge balance, water visibility) reduce stress through a safety signalling mechanism — that is, the landscape is preferred or stress-reducing because it signals absence of threats and availability of resources, not merely because it is pleasant to look at.
2. The study distinguishes between pleasantness/aesthetic preference and safety/threat appraisal as competing explanations for landscape preference or stress reduction, using separate measures for each.
3. The study manipulates landscape features associated with the savanna hypothesis (openness, refugia, visibility, resource cues) and measures either safety-related appraisals or physiological stress responses.
4. The study tests the evolutionary specificity claim — that savanna landscapes are preferred cross-culturally or in infants/young children who have not yet developed culturally learned preferences.

## Out-Criteria (Clear Rejections)

1. Papers about landscape aesthetics or scenic beauty ratings that do not measure or mention safety, threat appraisal, or stress — these are preference studies, not safety-signalling studies.
2. Papers about actual safety in urban design (e.g., crime prevention through environmental design, CPTED, streetlight effects on crime) — these are about real danger, not evolutionary safety signalling.
3. Papers about savanna ecology, paleoanthropology, or human evolution that do not include a psychological experiment — the evolutionary backstory is relevant context but not evidence for the mechanism.
4. Papers about fear conditioning or phobia treatment that do not involve landscape or environmental stimuli.

## Hard Cases (Borderline Decisions)

1. **Prospect-refuge studies without explicit "safety" framing**: Studies testing Appleton's prospect-refuge theory (preference for spaces offering both overview and enclosure) that measure preference but do not use the word "safety" or measure safety appraisal. Prospect-refuge is arguably a specification of safety signalling. *Tend toward: include if the theoretical framing connects prospect-refuge to threat/safety; edge case if it is purely about aesthetic preference.*

2. **Studies of "restorative" landscapes that happen to have savanna features**: A paper showing that open park landscapes reduce stress, where the landscape happens to have savanna-like features but is not framed in evolutionary terms. *Tend toward: edge case — the landscape may signal safety without the researcher explicitly testing that hypothesis.*

3. **Cross-cultural landscape preference studies**: Research showing that people across cultures prefer savanna-like landscapes. These provide indirect evidence for an evolved preference but do not test the mechanism (safety signalling vs. pleasantness vs. familiarity vs. processing fluency). *Tend toward: include as indirect evidence, flag that the mechanism is not tested.*

4. **Fear/threat studies in indoor environments**: Papers testing whether enclosed spaces or dim environments trigger threat responses, which is the inverse of the safety-signalling claim. *Tend toward: include if the theoretical framing draws the connection to landscape safety signalling; exclude if purely about indoor threat perception.*

## False Friends

- **"Savanna"** in ecological or biogeographical contexts — about the biome itself, not human psychological response to it
- **"Safety culture"** or **"safety climate"** — organizational psychology, not environmental psychology
- **"Signal" in neuroscience** — neural signalling, not environmental safety cues
- **"Landscape ecology"** — spatial patterns of ecosystems, not human perception of landscapes

## Evidence Markers

- Named theories: savanna hypothesis, prospect-refuge theory, habitat theory, biophobia/biophilia
- Safety or threat appraisal measures (safety ratings, perceived danger, threat detection tasks)
- Landscape feature manipulation: openness, tree density, visibility, water presence, refugia
- Cross-cultural or infant/child samples (testing evolutionary universality)
- References to Orians (1980, 1986), Appleton (1975, 1996), Ulrich (1983), Falk & Balling (2010)

## Escalation Triggers

1. **Papers testing landscape preference without separating the pleasantness and safety dimensions.** Most landscape preference studies measure only composite preference (liking), which conflates the two mechanisms this question aims to separate. The adjudicator must determine whether the study attempts to decompose preference into component appraisals.

2. **Papers from the "processing fluency" tradition** (Reber, Joye) that offer an alternative explanation for savanna preference. These argue that savanna landscapes are preferred because they are easy to process (high fluency), not because they signal safety. These are directly relevant as counter-evidence but will not contain the words "safety signalling."

3. **Papers on biophobia** (fear of snakes, spiders, heights) that provide evidence for evolved threat detection. These are the flip side of safety signalling — evolved sensitivity to danger cues. The adjudicator must decide whether biophobia evidence counts as indirect evidence for safety signalling in landscapes.

## Unresolved Ambiguities

- Is "safety signalling" a testable mechanism or an unfalsifiable narrative? If any landscape that reduces stress can be post-hoc described as "signalling safety," the hypothesis has no predictive power. The panel should prioritize studies that make specific predictions and can be falsified.
- How does cultural history interact with the evolutionary hypothesis? People from forested cultures may prefer forests over savannas, which challenges the universality claim without necessarily refuting the safety-signalling mechanism.

## LLM Drafting Note

This constitution was drafted with LLM panel reasoning. SQ-005 is the hardest question in the first 10 — the evidence base is genuinely sparse, and the central mechanism (safety signalling vs. pleasantness) is rarely tested directly. The constitution is designed to cast a wide net while flagging when a paper merely assumes safety signalling rather than testing it. Joye's inclusion ensures the competing "processing fluency" explanation is not ignored, while Bin Jiang (prospect-refuge fMRI) and Tybur (evolved threat detection) bring current neuroscience and evolutionary psychology methods to bear on a hypothesis that has traditionally relied on observational landscape preference data.
