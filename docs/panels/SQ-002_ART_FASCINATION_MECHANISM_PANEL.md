# Panel Dossier: SQ-002

## Question

What studies distinguish fascination as the active mechanism in attention restoration from mere relaxation or positive affect?

## Topic / Subtopic

Attention Restoration Theory / Mechanism

## In-Criteria (Central Cases)

A paper is clearly relevant to SQ-002 if:

1. The study explicitly attempts to isolate fascination (soft fascination, involuntary attention capture) as a mediator or mechanism in attention restoration, rather than treating nature exposure as an undifferentiated treatment.
2. The study measures both an attention outcome AND a fascination-related construct (e.g., rated fascination, Perceived Restorativeness Scale fascination subscale, involuntary attention measures) and tests whether fascination mediates the restoration effect.
3. The study contrasts fascination against a competing mechanism — such as positive mood, relaxation, reduced arousal, or distraction — to determine whether fascination adds explanatory power beyond affect.
4. The study manipulates fascination levels experimentally (e.g., comparing high-fascination nature scenes vs. low-fascination nature scenes while controlling for pleasantness) to test causality.

## Out-Criteria (Clear Rejections)

1. Papers that measure attention restoration after nature exposure but do not measure or manipulate fascination at all — these belong to SQ-001 (core evidence), not SQ-002 (mechanism).
2. Papers that study aesthetic preference for natural scenes without any cognitive or attention outcome — fascination as a concept appears in aesthetics, but without an attention measure, the paper does not address the mechanism question.
3. Papers about "fascination" in the colloquial sense (e.g., "fascination with technology," "fascination with violence") that have no connection to environmental psychology or attention theory.
4. Papers that use the Perceived Restorativeness Scale (PRS) but only report composite scores without isolating the fascination subscale — these confirm ART generally but do not test the mechanism question.

## Hard Cases (Borderline Decisions)

1. **PRS fascination subscale without mediation analysis**: A paper that reports PRS fascination scores as a dependent variable but does not test whether fascination mediates the attention restoration effect. This is component evidence (fascination is present in restorative environments) but not mechanism evidence (fascination causes the restoration). *Tend toward: include as indirect evidence, flag as incomplete mechanism test.*

2. **Nature exposure + mood + attention without mediation**: A study measuring mood and attention after nature exposure but not testing whether mood improvement explains the attention improvement (or whether fascination does). This is common in the literature. *Tend toward: include as relevant background, note that the mechanism question remains untested.*

3. **Mindfulness or meditation studies invoking "fascination"**: Some contemplative practice research uses language about "soft fascination" or "effortless attention" that overlaps with Kaplan's framework. These may be mechanistically informative even though the setting is not a natural environment. *Tend toward: include if the paper explicitly discusses ART or references Kaplan; exclude if the overlap is purely terminological.*

4. **Competing mechanism papers from SRT tradition**: Papers by Ulrich, van den Berg, or others arguing that stress reduction (not fascination) is the active ingredient. These directly address SQ-002 by attempting to falsify the fascination mechanism. *Tend toward: definitely include — they are the counter-evidence this question specifically seeks.*

## False Friends

- **"Fascinating results"** — colloquial usage in conclusions sections, not the construct
- **"Fascination with [topic X]"** — interest/curiosity, not Kaplanian fascination
- **"Captivating environments"** — may or may not relate to involuntary attention in the ART sense
- **"Engagement"** — sometimes a proxy for fascination but often means task engagement unrelated to ART

## Evidence Markers

- Perceived Restorativeness Scale (PRS), particularly fascination subscale
- Mediation analysis, path analysis, or structural equation model testing fascination as mediator
- Experimental manipulation of fascination level (e.g., high-fascination vs. low-fascination scenes matched on pleasantness)
- References to Kaplan's four ART properties: fascination, being away, extent, compatibility
- Phrases: "soft fascination," "involuntary attention," "effortless attention," "hard fascination"

## Escalation Triggers

1. **Papers measuring the PRS without subscale breakdown.** A keyword filter will flag papers mentioning "perceived restorativeness" but cannot determine whether the fascination subscale was separately analyzed. This requires reading the results section.

2. **Papers from the SRT tradition that implicitly challenge the fascination mechanism.** A paper arguing that autonomic stress recovery drives restoration is directly relevant to SQ-002 (it's the counter-evidence), but a keyword filter looking for "fascination" will miss it entirely. The adjudicator must recognize that mechanism-competition papers belong here even without the word "fascination."

3. **Papers using "restorative" in title but actually measuring a complex outcome bundle.** Many papers combine mood, attention, and physiological measures into a single "restorativeness" composite, making it impossible to determine from the abstract whether fascination was isolated.

## Unresolved Ambiguities

- Is fascination a single construct or a family of related processes? Some recent work distinguishes "soft fascination" (gentle interest) from "hard fascination" (riveting attention capture, e.g., watching a fire). Should both count?
- Should neuroimaging studies that show default mode network (DMN) activity during nature viewing be accepted as mechanism evidence for fascination, even if "fascination" is not named? The neural signature may be more precise than the self-report construct.

## LLM Drafting Note

This constitution was drafted with LLM panel reasoning. The key intellectual challenge is that SQ-002 asks about a specific mechanism, but most of the ART literature tests the overall theory rather than isolating fascination. The constitution is therefore deliberately inclusive of near-misses and competing-mechanism papers, because the interesting cases are precisely the ones that attempt — or fail — to isolate the mechanism.
