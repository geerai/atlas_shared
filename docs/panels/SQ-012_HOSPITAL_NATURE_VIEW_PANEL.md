# Panel Dossier: SQ-012

## Question

What evidence shows that hospital patients with window views of nature recover faster or report lower pain than those with views of walls or urban scenes?

## Topic / Subtopic

Views of Nature / Hospital recovery

## In-Criteria (Central Cases)

1. The study tests whether hospital patients with window views of natural scenes (trees, gardens, sky, greenery) recover faster (shorter length of stay), report lower pain, or use fewer analgesics compared to patients with views of walls, built structures, or no windows.
2. The study is conducted in a real clinical setting (hospital, rehabilitation facility, surgical recovery ward) with actual patient recovery as the outcome.
3. The study uses a quasi-experimental or controlled-comparison design where window view is the primary differentiating variable, with other aspects of care held approximately constant.
4. Systematic reviews or meta-analyses pooling multiple hospital nature-view studies with patient recovery outcomes.

## Out-Criteria (Clear Rejections)

1. Papers about nature exposure in non-clinical settings (offices, classrooms, outdoor recreation). These belong to SQ-001, SQ-003, or SQ-008.
2. Papers about hospital design focused on infection control, acoustics, wayfinding, or general facility layout without a nature-view component.
3. Papers about nature as a therapeutic modality (horticultural therapy, garden therapy programs) where the activity — not the view — is the intervention.
4. Papers about art in hospitals (nature paintings, murals, projected images) unless the study explicitly compares these to actual window views.

## Hard Cases (Borderline Decisions)

1. **Nature paintings or photographs in patient rooms**: Studies comparing nature art on walls to no art. These test nature visual content but not the window-view mechanism specifically. *Tend toward: include — the visual exposure pathway is similar, and the art-vs-window comparison is itself informative.*

2. **Healing gardens and outdoor access**: Studies of hospital gardens where patients physically enter the garden. This involves nature exposure but goes beyond window-view (add mobility, fresh air, social interaction). *Tend toward: edge case — include if the study isolates the visual component or compares garden-view from room to garden-walk.*

3. **Simulated nature views (screens, projections)**: Studies installing nature video or projected images as substitutes for windows. *Tend toward: include — directly relevant as a design alternative to real windows; also relevant to SQ-013 cross-reference.*

4. **Studies measuring clinician or staff outcomes rather than patient outcomes**: Does the nature view benefit the healthcare workers? *Tend toward: edge case — the question specifies patients, but staff outcomes in healthcare settings are closely related.*

5. **Neonatal or pediatric ICU studies**: Nature views or imagery for infants or very young children. The recovery mechanisms may differ substantially from adult surgical patients. *Tend toward: include with annotation — the population is distinct from Ulrich's original sample.*

## False Friends

- **"Window of opportunity"** — timing concept, not architectural window
- **"Nature of recovery"** — character of recovery, not natural scenery
- **"View" in epidemiology** — perspective or opinion, not visual window view
- **"Patient view"** — patient opinion or perspective survey
- **"Recovery room"** — post-anesthesia care, not view-related

## Evidence Markers

- Length of stay (LOS) as outcome
- Analgesic use, pain medication consumption
- Patient pain ratings (VAS, NRS)
- Nurse notes on patient complaints or emotional states
- Window orientation or view classification as independent variable
- References to Ulrich (1984), Verderber (1986), Beauchemin & Hays (1996)
- Hospital, surgical, rehabilitation, clinical setting description

## Escalation Triggers

The following cases MUST be escalated to a subscribed LLM adjudicator rather than resolved by keyword heuristics:

1. **Multi-factor renovation studies** where the nature view improvement is bundled with other changes (lighting, layout, noise reduction). The nature-view contribution cannot be isolated. The adjudicator must accept but flag the confound and assess whether the view change is the primary renovation feature or incidental.

2. **Studies using recovery metrics from different medical specialties.** A cardiac surgery study and an orthopaedic surgery study may report different recovery indicators. The adjudicator must determine whether the outcomes are comparable for pooling or represent distinct recovery constructs.

3. **Studies of nature imagery (paintings, photographs, videos) in patient rooms without real window views.** The adjudicator must classify these as testing a related but distinct mechanism from SQ-012's core question and cross-reference SQ-013 (virtual nature).

## Unresolved Ambiguities

- Ulrich (1984) studied surgical patients recovering from cholecystectomy. How far do findings generalize to other patient populations (medical, psychiatric, rehabilitation, chronic)?
- The "view" mechanism may involve light exposure (circadian, vitamin D) in addition to visual nature content. Studies rarely isolate the visual-information pathway from the light-exposure pathway.

## LLM Drafting Note

This constitution was drafted with LLM panel reasoning. SQ-012 is anchored by one of the most cited studies in environmental psychology (Ulrich 1984, N=46). The constitution must be careful to include replication attempts, failures, and methodological critiques — the original study had a small sample and no randomization. Including Baird (clinical methodology) and Joye (alternative mechanisms) provides critical balance.
