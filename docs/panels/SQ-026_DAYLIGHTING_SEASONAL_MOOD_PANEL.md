# Panel Dossier: SQ-026

## Question

What evidence shows that daylighting strategies in buildings reduce seasonal mood changes or depressive symptoms beyond clinical SAD populations?

## Topic / Subtopic

## In-Criteria (Central Cases)

1. The study tests whether daylighting strategies in buildings (window size, orientation, light shelves, skylights, atria, dynamic shading) reduce seasonal mood variation, depressive symptoms, or improve psychological wellbeing in non-clinical populations.
2. The study compares occupants of well-daylit buildings/rooms to occupants of poorly daylit buildings/rooms on mood or depressive symptom measures.
3. The study measures seasonal variation in mood or cognition as a function of building daylighting quality — does adequate daylight buffer against winter mood decline?
4. Large-scale epidemiological studies linking building daylight exposure to population-level mental health indicators.

## Out-Criteria (Clear Rejections)

1. Papers about light therapy for clinically diagnosed SAD using light boxes. Clinical phototherapy is a medical treatment, not a building design question.
2. Papers about daylighting for energy savings, visual comfort, or glare management without mood/mental health outcomes.
3. Papers about outdoor light exposure (time spent outdoors) without building daylighting as the variable.
4. Papers about circadian lighting with tunable LEDs (artificial spectrum management). These belong to SQ-016.

## Hard Cases (Borderline Decisions)

1. **Studies of natural daylight in hospitals reducing depression in inpatients**: Clinical populations, but the intervention is architectural (room orientation, window design). *Tend toward: include — the question asks about building design, and hospital patients receiving more daylight represent a daylighting strategy test.*

2. **Beauchemin & Hays (1996) and similar**: Classic study showing bipolar patients in sunny rooms had shorter hospital stays. *Tend toward: include — this is foundational evidence for daylighting and mood, even in a clinical setting.*

3. **Studies of daylighting in schools affecting student mood/behavior**: Students are non-clinical, and the setting is a building. *Tend toward: include — squarely within SQ-026.*

4. **Dawn simulation studies in the home**: Devices mimicking gradual dawn light in bedrooms. *Tend toward: edge case — this is a light intervention with mood outcomes, but it is not a daylighting strategy in the architectural sense.*

5. **Studies using daylight autonomy metrics without mood outcomes**: Papers reporting spatial daylight autonomy (sDA) or useful daylight illuminance (UDI) for buildings without occupant psychological data. *Tend toward: reject — the daylight measurement is relevant but without mood/mental health outcomes it does not address SQ-026.*

## False Friends

- **"Daylight saving time"** — clock change, not architectural daylighting
- **"Seasonal"** in marketing — seasonal product trends
- **"Light"** in physics — optical properties without human health
- **"Mood lighting"** — decorative ambiance, not evidence-based daylight design
- **"SAD" as acronym** — Social Anxiety Disorder (also SAD), not Seasonal Affective Disorder

## Evidence Markers

- Daylight metrics: da (daylight autonomy), sDA (spatial daylight autonomy), UDI, DF (daylight factor)
- Window characteristics: window-to-wall ratio, orientation (north/south), glazing type
- Mood instruments: BDI, CES-D, PHQ-9, POMS depression subscale, SIGH-SAD
- Seasonal measures: tracking across months or seasons, winter vs. summer comparisons
- References to Heschong (2002), Beauchemin & Hays (1996), Wirz-Justice et al. (2005), Terman & Terman (2005), Boubekri et al. (2014, 2020), van Someren et al. (2022), Wienold et al. (2019)

## Escalation Triggers

The following cases MUST be escalated to a subscribed LLM adjudicator rather than resolved by keyword heuristics:

1. **Studies showing that daylighting benefits disappear at higher latitudes** where winter daylight is extremely limited. The adjudicator must determine whether the latitude effect represents a genuine boundary condition for daylighting design or merely reflects insufficient daylight dose, and flag the implication for design guidelines above 55°N/S.

2. **Studies comparing daylighting to electric melanopic lighting** — does architectural daylight provide benefits that electric light cannot replicate? The adjudicator must assess whether the study controlled for illuminance and spectral match, and determine whether any daylight-specific benefit is attributable to the view component, the dynamic spectrum, or the full-spectrum dose.

3. **Studies of daylighting mediating exercise, social interaction, or sleep quality** as the pathway to mood improvements (rather than direct photobiological effects). The adjudicator must assess the mediation analysis quality and determine whether the indirect pathway reduces the direct daylighting → mood effect to non-significance.

## Unresolved Ambiguities

- Whether the mood benefit of daylight is photobiological (retinal melanopsin → circadian → serotonin/melatonin) or psychological (view, connection to outdoors, time awareness) is rarely tested.
- "Non-clinical" is a continuum — subclinical seasonal mood variation is common, and the boundary with SAD is arbitrary.

## LLM Drafting Note

This constitution was drafted with LLM panel reasoning. SQ-026 bridges the clinical SAD literature and the architectural daylighting literature — two communities that rarely cite each other. The panel combines Heschong (foundational daylighting) and Mardaljevic (metric engineering) with Boubekri (daylight-sleep-cognition in offices), van Someren (light-mood neuroscience), and Wienold (dynamic glare-comfort metrics) to ensure both clinical translation and building-science rigor.
