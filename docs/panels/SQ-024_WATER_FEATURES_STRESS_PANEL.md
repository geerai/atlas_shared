# Panel Dossier: SQ-024

## Question

What evidence shows that water features (fountains, indoor water walls, water sounds) reduce stress or improve wellbeing in built environments?

## Topic / Subtopic

## In-Criteria (Central Cases)

1. The study tests whether visual, auditory, or multisensory exposure to water features (fountains, indoor water walls, aquariums, water sounds, stream/waterfall imagery) reduces stress or improves wellbeing in a built environment context.
2. The study compares a water-feature condition to a non-water control condition using stress, mood, or physiological outcomes.
3. The study examines water features in interior design contexts — lobbies, atriums, hospital waiting areas, offices, residential spaces.
4. Epidemiological studies of blue space (coastal proximity, river access) and mental health, analogous to green space epidemiology.

## Out-Criteria (Clear Rejections)

1. Papers about water quality, drinking water safety, or water infrastructure engineering.
2. Papers about hydrotherapy, aquatic exercise, or swimming for physical rehabilitation without environmental stress outcomes.
3. Papers about flood risk, water pollution, or water-related natural hazards.
4. Papers about marine biology or aquatic ecology without human psychological outcomes.

## Hard Cases (Borderline Decisions)

1. **Aquarium studies in clinical settings**: Cracknell's research on aquarium viewing for dental/medical anxiety. These are clinical populations. *Tend toward: include — the mechanism (visual water feature reducing stress) generalizes to built environment design.*

2. **Nature soundscape studies where water sounds are one of several stimuli**: Studies testing birdsong, water, wind, and rain together. *Tend toward: include if water sound effects are reported separately; edge case if bundled with other nature sounds.*

3. **Blue space epidemiology (coastal proximity)**: Studies of mental health and distance to coast or rivers. These are outdoor/landscape scale, not indoor water features. *Tend toward: include as supporting evidence for the blue-space principle, but flag that the mechanism may differ from indoor water features.*

4. **Virtual water features**: VR or screen-based simulated water features. *Tend toward: include — same logic as SQ-013 for virtual nature generally.*

5. **Water feature noise masking in offices**: Fountains used primarily for acoustic masking of speech. *Tend toward: include if stress or wellbeing is measured; edge case if only acoustic metrics (STI, dB reduction) are reported.*

## Evidence Markers

- Water feature type: fountain, water wall, aquarium, stream, waterfall, pond
- Sensory modality: visual water, auditory water sounds, multimodal
- Stress outcomes: cortisol, heart rate, SCL, self-reported stress, STAI
- Wellbeing outcomes: POMS, WEMWBS, SF-36 MCS
- Blue space metrics: distance to coast, area of visible water
- References to White et al. (2010, 2013, 2020), Cracknell et al. (2016), Barton & Pretty (2010)

## Escalation Triggers

The following cases MUST be escalated to a subscribed LLM adjudicator rather than resolved by keyword heuristics:

1. **Studies comparing water feature modalities** — is the visual, auditory, or thermal (humidity, air cooling) component most important? The adjudicator must determine whether the study isolates individual sensory channels or tests them in combination, and classify the study by which modality is the primary independent variable.

2. **Studies of negative water responses**: Some people find water sounds (dripping, rushing) anxiety-provoking or associating with urgency. The adjudicator must determine whether these individual differences represent genuine counter-evidence to the general restorative claim or reflect specific subpopulations (e.g., people with incontinence concerns, tinnitus sensitivity).

3. **Economic valuation studies**: Property values, willingness to pay for water views. The adjudicator must determine whether the economic valuation provides indirect evidence of perceived health benefit or is purely a market preference study without health implications, and classify accordingly.

## Unresolved Ambiguities

- Indoor water features may increase humidity and produce acoustic noise that interferes with speech. The design tradeoffs are rarely studied alongside benefits.
- Whether the benefit is about "water" (biophilic affinity) or about "movement" (dynamic visual stimulation, white noise masking) is poorly disentangled.
