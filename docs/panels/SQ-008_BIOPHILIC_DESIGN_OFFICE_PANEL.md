# Panel Dossier: SQ-008

## Question

What design features — plants, water, natural light, natural materials — show the strongest evidence for reducing stress or improving wellbeing in offices?

## Topic / Subtopic

Biophilia / Design translation

## In-Criteria (Central Cases)

1. The study tests the effect of a specific biophilic design feature (indoor plants, water features, natural light/daylight, natural materials like wood or stone, nature views, green walls, nature imagery) on self-reported stress, objectively measured stress, or self-reported wellbeing in an office, workplace, or commercial interior setting.
2. The study uses a controlled or quasi-experimental design comparing a workspace with the design feature present versus absent, or comparing different levels of the feature (e.g., many plants vs. few plants).
3. Post-occupancy evaluation (POE) studies of offices that specifically isolate the contribution of biophilic design features to occupant-reported wellbeing or stress outcomes.
4. Meta-analyses or systematic reviews pooling evidence across multiple biophilic design interventions in workplace settings.

## Out-Criteria (Clear Rejections)

1. Papers about biophilic design theory, philosophy, or advocacy with no empirical data — advocacy documents, design pattern books, and case study descriptions without measured outcomes.
2. Papers about energy-efficient or "green" office design that measure only energy consumption, carbon footprint, or material sustainability with no occupant psychological outcomes.
3. Papers about residential, hospital, or school environments — the question specifies offices. (These may be relevant to SQ-003 or SQ-012.)
4. Papers about outdoor workplace environments (outdoor offices, co-working in parks) — the question is about interior design features.

## Hard Cases (Borderline Decisions)

1. **Multi-feature interventions**: A study testing an office renovation that adds plants, improves daylight, and changes acoustic treatment simultaneously. The biophilic elements cannot be isolated. *Tend toward: include with caveat — the study informs the question but cannot identify which feature is responsible.*

2. **"Productivity" as the outcome**: Many office studies measure "productivity" rather than "stress" or "wellbeing." Productivity may involve cognitive performance (relevant) or output quantity (less relevant). *Tend toward: include if productivity is operationalized through a behavioral or cognitive measure; edge case if it is a single self-report item.*

3. **Nature view from office window**: Studies of window views (Ulrich-adjacent). Are window views a "design feature" or an "environmental condition"? *Tend toward: include — the architect can specify window size, orientation, and view content, making it a designable feature.*

4. **Simulated daylight (tunable LEDs)**: Studies testing circadian-aligned artificial lighting that simulates natural light dynamics. This is nature-inspired but not actually natural light. *Tend toward: include — the design feature is motivated by biophilia and addresses the same outcome (daylight-like benefits in offices).*

5. **Home office / remote work studies**: With remote work increasing, some studies test biophilic design in home offices. The question specifies "offices" — does "home office" count? *Tend toward: edge case — include if the setting is described as a dedicated workspace, exclude if it is a living room used for occasional work.*

## False Friends

- **"Office plants"** in air-quality studies — sometimes the outcome is only VOC removal or CO₂ levels with no psychological measure
- **"Natural light"** in energy performance studies — illuminance, glare, and cooling load, not occupant wellbeing
- **"Workplace wellbeing"** — sometimes refers to HR programs (mindfulness training, EAP), not physical design
- **"Green office"** — sustainability certification, not biophilic design

## Evidence Markers

- Named biophilic features: indoor plants, green walls, living walls, water features, daylight, natural materials (wood, stone, bamboo), nature views, nature artwork/imagery
- Wellbeing measures: GHQ, WHO-5, stress scales (PSS, DASS), mood (PANAS, Profile of Mood States)
- Workplace-specific outcomes: job satisfaction, sick leave, absenteeism, self-rated productivity
- Physiological measures in office settings: ambulatory cortisol, wearable HR/HRV
- References to Browning et al. (2014) 14 Patterns, Heerwagen, Kellert

## Escalation Triggers

1. **Papers listing "biophilic design" in their keywords but measuring only satisfaction or preference, not stress or wellbeing.** Preference for an office with plants is not the same as stress reduction from plants. The adjudicator must verify the outcome is stress or wellbeing, not just liking.

2. **Papers studying daylight effects that could be circadian rather than biophilic.** Daylight improves alertness and sleep quality through the melanopic pathway (circadian mechanism), not necessarily through a biophilic pathway. The adjudicator should accept these but flag the mechanism ambiguity.

3. **Papers comparing multiple design features to identify "which works best."** These are the most valuable for SQ-008 but rarest. The adjudicator should prioritize these for review.

## Unresolved Ambiguities

- Should "strongest evidence" be assessed by effect size, study quality, or replication count? A single high-quality RCT of indoor plants might be more informative than 20 poorly controlled surveys.
- How should dose be handled? One small potted plant vs. a full living wall represent very different "doses" of the same design feature. Should dose-response studies be prioritized?
