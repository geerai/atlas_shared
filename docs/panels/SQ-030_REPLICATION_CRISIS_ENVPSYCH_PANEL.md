# Panel Dossier: SQ-030

## Question

How well have the foundational environmental psychology findings of Ulrich (1984) and Kaplan & Kaplan (1989) replicated with larger or more diverse samples?

## Topic / Subtopic

Replication Crisis / Environmental psychology

## In-Criteria (Central Cases)

1. The study is an explicit replication or close replication of Ulrich (1984) or Kaplan & Kaplan (1989) — using the same or closely matched paradigm with a new, larger, or more diverse sample.
2. The study tests the same core predictions (nature view → faster recovery, nature exposure → attention restoration) with substantially larger N than the originals (Ulrich N=46; most early ART studies N<100).
3. Meta-analyses or systematic reviews estimating the overall effect size for the claims in the founding studies, assessing publication bias, and evaluating heterogeneity.
4. Papers that explicitly address the replication or robustness of foundational claims in environmental psychology as a methodological or meta-scientific question.

## Out-Criteria (Clear Rejections)

1. Papers about the replication crisis in other fields (social psychology priming, ego depletion) without environmental psychology application.
2. Papers that cite Ulrich or Kaplan as background but are testing a different question entirely.
3. Papers about nature-health associations that do not reference replication, robustness, or the foundational studies specifically.
4. Papers about environmental psychology theory development without replication data.

## Hard Cases (Borderline Decisions)

1. **Large-N epidemiological studies providing indirect replication**: Studies like White et al. (2013, N=20,000) testing whether reported time in nature predicts wellbeing. These are not direct replications but provide large-sample evidence for the core claims. *Tend toward: include — large-sample evidence from different paradigms provides convergent validity even without exact replication.*

2. **Studies in non-Western or non-WEIRD populations**: Tests of ART or SRT in East Asian, African, or South American samples. These are conceptual replications with diverse populations. *Tend toward: include — population diversity is a specific dimension of replication quality.*

3. **Studies finding smaller effect sizes than originally reported**: Papers where the nature-restoration effect exists but is substantially smaller than Ulrich (1984) or early ART studies suggested. *Tend toward: definitely include — effect-size shrinkage between original and replication is a key replication metric.*

4. **Pre-registered or registered-report nature studies**: These represent improved methodology applied to foundational claims. *Tend toward: include — pre-registration addresses publication bias, which is a core replication concern.*

5. **Studies critiquing the methodology of the originals without new data**: Papers pointing out limitations (small N, quasi-experimental, confounds) of Ulrich (1984) or Kaplan studies. *Tend toward: include — methodological critique is part of the replication assessment even without new data.*

## False Friends

- **"Replication"** in molecular biology — DNA replication
- **"Foundation"** in architecture — structural foundation
- **"Environment"** in computing — software environment
- **"Recovery"** in data science — data recovery
- **"Diverse samples"** in statistics — sample diversity for estimation

## Evidence Markers

- Explicit "replication" language in title or abstract
- Power analysis, sample size justification, pre-registration
- Effect size comparison with original study (original d= vs. replication d=)
- Publication bias analysis: funnel plots, p-curve, trim-and-fill
- Heterogeneity statistics (I², Q) in meta-analytic context
- References to Ulrich (1984), Kaplan & Kaplan (1989), Kaplan (1995), Hartig et al. (2003, 2014)
- Open science indicators: pre-registration, open data, open materials

## Escalation Triggers

The following cases MUST be escalated to a subscribed LLM adjudicator rather than resolved by keyword heuristics:

1. **Failed replications** — studies that find no significant nature-restoration or nature-recovery effect using adequate power. The adjudicator must assess whether the study had sufficient statistical power (≥80% for the expected effect size), used comparable methodology to the original, and report the comparison of observed vs. expected effect sizes. These are the highest-value findings for SQ-030.

2. **Publication bias evidence**: p-curve or funnel plot analyses suggesting that the environmental psychology literature has inflated effect sizes due to selective reporting. The adjudicator must assess the statistical validity of the bias analysis and determine whether the estimated bias-corrected effect size still supports the foundational claims.

3. **Studies using physiological and cognitive measures simultaneously** in large samples — these provide the richest replication data because they test both ART and SRT predictions at once. The adjudicator must determine sample size adequacy and whether the study is pre-registered, and flag for cross-reference with SQ-001 (ART core), SQ-004 (SRT physiology), and SQ-006 (ART vs SRT confound).

## Unresolved Ambiguities

- What counts as a "replication" is contested. Many nature studies test the same general idea (nature improves wellbeing) with different stimuli, measures, and populations. Whether these are replications or new studies is a judgment call.
- Ulrich (1984) and Kaplan & Kaplan (1989) are very different studies — one is a clinical record study, the other is a theoretical book. "Replicating" a theory book is conceptually different from replicating a specific experiment.
- The field may have a file-drawer problem — many null environmental-psychology experiments may never have been published.

This constitution was drafted with LLM panel reasoning. SQ-030 is the meta-scientific capstone of the bank. It asks whether the field's foundational evidence is trustworthy. Including Nosek (reproducibility movement) alongside Hartig and van den Berg (who have actually tested these claims repeatedly) ensures both meta-scientific methods expertise and domain knowledge.
