# Panel Dossier: SQ-015

## Question

How does noise from open-plan offices affect cognitive performance and stress, and what acoustic interventions most reduce the harm?

## Topic / Subtopic

Acoustic Environment / Open-plan offices

## In-Criteria (Central Cases)

1. The study measures cognitive performance (concentration, memory, reasoning, proofreading, creative tasks) and/or physiological/psychological stress (cortisol, self-reported stress, cardiovascular measures) in open-plan office environments, with noise as the primary independent variable or key predictor.
2. The study tests acoustic interventions (sound masking, absorptive materials, acoustic screens, personal noise-cancelling devices, spatial planning, speech privacy solutions) and their effect on cognitive performance or stress in open-plan settings.
3. The study compares performance or wellbeing across office types (open-plan vs. private, open-plan vs. shared, activity-based) with acoustic conditions as a differentiating factor.
4. Laboratory studies using simulated open-plan office noise (irrelevant speech, typing, phone conversations, HVAC noise) to test speech intelligibility effects on cognition.

## Out-Criteria (Clear Rejections)

1. Papers about industrial noise exposure and hearing loss without cognitive or stress outcomes.
2. Papers about residential or neighborhood noise (traffic, aircraft) at home rather than in the workplace.
3. Papers about classroom acoustics and student performance. These are relevant to acoustics generally but the setting (school) and population (children) distinguish them from SQ-015.
4. Papers about office design focusing on lighting, thermal comfort, or layout without acoustic content.

## Hard Cases (Borderline Decisions)

1. **Home office / remote work acoustics**: Studies of noise distraction during work-from-home. The acoustic challenges differ from open-plan (domestic noise vs. irrelevant speech). *Tend toward: edge case — include if the study compares home noise to open-plan noise or uses standards relevant to open-plan.*

2. **Activity-based working (ABW) studies**: Modern flexible offices where workers choose zones. These are not traditional open-plan but share acoustic characteristics. *Tend toward: include — ABW represents the evolution of open-plan design and directly addresses the intervention question.*

3. **Studies measuring preference or satisfaction but not cognitive performance**: Large-scale surveys (e.g., CBE, Leesman) measuring acoustic satisfaction. *Tend toward: edge case — satisfaction is an important outcome but does not directly demonstrate cognitive or stress effects.*

4. **Background music studies in offices**: Whether ambient music helps or hinders performance in open-plan settings. *Tend toward: include if the music is framed as an acoustic intervention; reject if it is a general music-and-cognition study without office context.*

5. **Phonological loop / irrelevant sound effect studies in lab settings**: Cognitive psychology studies demonstrating that changing-state speech disrupts serial recall. *Tend toward: include only if the study connects findings to open-plan office implications. Pure cognitive psychology without workplace framing belongs to basic memory research.*

## False Friends

- **"Open plan"** in urban planning — open public spaces, not offices
- **"Acoustic design"** in concert halls — performance venue optimization
- **"Noise cancellation"** in signal processing — electronic engineering, not occupant wellbeing
- **"Office environment"** studies focusing on thermal or lighting without acoustic component
- **"Speech intelligibility"** in audiology — hearing aid assessment

## Evidence Markers

- Specific acoustic metrics: Speech Transmission Index (STI), distraction distance, A-weighted sound pressure level (dB(A)), reverberation time (RT60)
- Irrelevant speech paradigm, changing-state effect
- Office typology: open-plan, cell office, shared office, activity-based
- Cognitive tasks: serial recall, proofreading, creative tasks, complex arithmetic
- Self-reported measures: acoustic satisfaction, perceived productivity, distraction frequency
- References to Jahncke et al. (2011), Hongisto et al. (2016), Haapakangas et al. (2014), Banbury & Berry (1998)

## Escalation Triggers

The following cases MUST be escalated to a subscribed LLM adjudicator rather than resolved by keyword heuristics:

1. **Studies finding that some individuals perform better with moderate background noise** (e.g., the "coffee shop effect" or creative tasks enhanced by moderate noise). The adjudicator must determine whether the study distinguishes structured background noise (white noise, nature sounds) from intelligible speech noise, and assess whether the benefit applies to creative tasks only or generalizes to concentration-demanding work.

2. **Studies comparing different speech masking systems** (pink noise, nature sounds, processed speech babble). The adjudicator must assess whether the study isolates the acoustic masking effectiveness from the psychological acceptability of the masking sound, and determine which masking signal type yields the best performance-satisfaction trade-off.

3. **Studies measuring long-term health outcomes** (sick leave, burnout, chronic stress markers) related to open-plan noise exposure, not just acute cognitive performance. The adjudicator must determine whether confounders (job type, organizational culture, personal factors) are adequately controlled and assess whether the chronic outcome can be attributed specifically to acoustic exposure rather than open-plan design generally.

## Unresolved Ambiguities

- Is the primary mechanism of open-plan noise harm speech intelligibility (hearing and processing intelligible speech) or general arousal/distraction (any noise increase)?
- Individual differences — introversion/extraversion, noise sensitivity — moderate the effect substantially but are rarely controlled across studies.
- The distinction between "controllable" noise (one's own activities) and "uncontrollable" noise (others' conversations) is critical but difficult to operationalize.

## LLM Drafting Note

This constitution was drafted with LLM panel reasoning. SQ-015 is the most practically important acoustic question — affecting hundreds of millions of office workers worldwide. The main challenge is that the literature spans three distinct communities (cognitive psychology lab studies, acoustic engineering field measurements, occupational health surveys) using different methods and vocabularies. Including Jahncke (lab/field bridge), Hongisto (engineering), Jones (cognitive mechanisms), and Danielsson (workplace design) ensures coverage across all three.
