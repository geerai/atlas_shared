# Panel Dossier: SQ-029

## Question

How does cultural background shape preferences for natural versus built environments, and what are the implications for universal design guidelines?

## Topic / Subtopic

Cultural Variation / Nature preference

## In-Criteria (Central Cases)

1. The study compares preferences for natural versus built environments across two or more cultural or national groups, using similar methodology.
2. The study tests whether nature preference findings from Western (typically North American or Northern European) populations replicate in non-Western populations.
3. The study tests whether restorative effects of nature (attention, stress, mood) vary by cultural background, acculturation, or nature-exposure history.
4. Studies examining culturally specific nature practices (shinrin-yoku, fēng shuǐ, Islamic garden tradition, Aboriginal landscape values) and their psychological implications.

## Out-Criteria (Clear Rejections)

1. Papers about cultural variation in topics unrelated to environmental preference (food, music, social norms).
2. Papers about landscape ecology or environmental management across countries without psychological preference data.
3. Papers about cross-cultural psychology that do not involve environmental or nature stimuli.
4. Papers about multicultural architecture or "culturally sensitive design" without empirical preference testing.

## Hard Cases (Borderline Decisions)

1. **Urban density preference studies**: Cultural variation in preferred urban density (European plaza vs. American suburb vs. Asian high-rise). *Tend toward: include if the study compares nature elements or greenness across city forms; edge case if it concerns density without nature content.*

2. **Immigrant acculturation studies**: Do immigrants adopt the nature preferences of their new country or retain those of their origin culture? *Tend toward: include — acculturation studies directly test whether preference is innate or learned.*

3. **Religious or spiritual landscape meaning studies**: Gardens in Islam, gardens in Buddhism, sacred natural spaces. *Tend toward: include if preference or wellbeing outcomes are measured; edge case if the study is purely interpretive without empirical data.*

4. **Socioeconomic confounds**: Low-income vs. high-income populations may differ in nature access and preference. Is this cultural or economic? *Tend toward: include if culture and SES are disentangled or if the study acknowledges the confound.*

5. **Children's nature preferences across cultures**: Developmental studies comparing children's landscape preferences across countries. *Tend toward: include — children are useful for testing innate vs. culturally learned preferences (cross-references SQ-007).*

## False Friends

- **"Cultural background"** in microbiology — growth medium for bacteria
- **"Natural preference"** in economics — revealed preference theory
- **"Built environment"** in civil engineering — structural engineering
- **"Universal design"** in accessibility — disability accommodation, not cross-cultural design
- **"Design guidelines"** in software — coding standards

## Evidence Markers

- Cross-cultural sample: at least two national or cultural groups explicitly compared
- Standardized cross-cultural methods: same stimuli, same scales, translated instruments
- Nature preference instruments: EPS, PRS, landscape preference rating
- Cultural variables: national origin, acculturation scale, nature-exposure history, urbanization
- References to Kaplan (2007), Korpela (2002), Yang & Brown (1992), Buijs et al. (2009)

## Escalation Triggers

The following cases MUST be escalated to a subscribed LLM adjudicator rather than resolved by keyword heuristics:

1. **Studies finding that specific nature features (fractals, water, flowers) are universally preferred** while others (lawn, forest type) are culturally specific. The adjudicator must assess whether the study uses comparable stimuli across cultural groups and determine where the universal-vs-specific boundary falls.

2. **Studies from regions heavily underrepresented in environmental psychology**: Africa, South America, South and Southeast Asia, the Middle East. The adjudicator must flag these for their generalizability value and determine whether the study's methods are comparable to the Western-origin studies being extended.

3. **Studies challenging the biophilia hypothesis by showing purely cultural preference patterns** — if nature preference is entirely constructed, universal design guidelines are inappropriate. The adjudicator must assess whether the study distinguishes genuine cultural construction from cultural modulation of an underlying universal tendency, and cross-reference SQ-007 (biophilia innate vs. learned).

## Unresolved Ambiguities

- "Culture" is a fuzzy variable — national borders do not cleanly delineate cultural groups, and within-country variation may exceed between-country variation.
- The implication for "universal design guidelines" is itself contested — even if preferences vary, buildings may need to serve diverse populations in the same space.

## LLM Drafting Note

This constitution was drafted with LLM panel reasoning. SQ-029 is the broadest cross-cultural question in the bank. The main risk is that few studies achieve genuine cross-cultural comparison (same methods, different populations) — most are single-culture studies that note their own cultural context. The panel is led by Scannell (place attachment and cross-cultural nature-identity) alongside Hwang (Asian urban nature) and Korpela (Nordic restoration cultures) to ensure the constitution goes beyond the Western-centric default. Ward Thompson provides the social-inclusion lens for green space access across cultural groups.
