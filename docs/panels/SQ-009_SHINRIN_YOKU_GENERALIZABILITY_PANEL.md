# Panel Dossier: SQ-009

## Question

How well do findings from Japanese forest-bathing (shinrin-yoku) studies generalise to Western or urban populations?

## Topic / Subtopic

Biophilia / Population transfer

## In-Criteria (Central Cases)

1. The study replicates a Japanese shinrin-yoku protocol (or close variant) in a non-Japanese population and measures comparable physiological or psychological outcomes, allowing direct comparison of effect sizes.
2. The study compares shinrin-yoku effects across two or more cultural or demographic populations within the same design (e.g., Japanese vs. Finnish participants, urban vs. rural residents).
3. The study systematically reviews or meta-analyzes the shinrin-yoku literature and addresses population generalizability as a research question — are effect sizes moderated by country, culture, or urbanization level?
4. Adaptation studies that modify the shinrin-yoku protocol for Western populations (e.g., guided forest therapy walks in North American or European forests) and test whether the modified protocol produces comparable effects.

## Out-Criteria (Clear Rejections)

1. Shinrin-yoku studies conducted only in Japanese populations with no discussion of generalizability — these provide the base evidence but do not address SQ-009's transfer question.
2. General nature-exposure studies in Western populations that do not reference or replicate the shinrin-yoku paradigm — a study of nature walks in British parks, unless it explicitly compares to the Japanese protocol, belongs to SQ-001 or SQ-004.
3. Papers about forest ecology, forestry management, or timber production in Japan that use the word "forest" but have no psychological or physiological component.
4. Tourism or recreational studies of Japanese forests that measure only visitor satisfaction or economic impact.

## Hard Cases (Borderline Decisions)

1. **European/American nature-walk studies that closely resemble shinrin-yoku but don't cite it**: A study of 30-minute guided walks in a German forest measuring cortisol and mood, without referencing Li or Miyazaki. The protocol is essentially shinrin-yoku. *Tend toward: include if the protocol is comparable in duration, setting, and measures — the generalizability question is answered by the data, regardless of labelling.*

2. **Korean or Chinese forest-bathing studies**: These are East Asian but not Japanese. Do they address the cross-cultural question? *Tend toward: include as partial evidence for cultural breadth, but note that Korean/Chinese/Japanese populations may share Confucian cultural attitudes toward nature that limit the cross-cultural generalizability claim.*

3. **Virtual shinrin-yoku studies**: Studies testing whether VR forest environments produce shinrin-yoku-like effects in Western participants. These test the protocol in a new population but also change the stimulus modality. *Tend toward: edge case — include if VR forest walks are a deliberate adaptation of shinrin-yoku for populations without forest access.*

4. **NK cell activity studies in non-Japanese populations**: Li's foundational work showed forest exposure increases NK cell activity. If a European study replicates the NK cell finding, this is strong generalizability evidence — but NK cell measurement is not standard in most Western nature studies. *Tend toward: include as high-value transfer evidence.*

## False Friends

- **"Forest management"** or **"silviculture"** — forestry, not human health
- **"Bathing"** in dermatology or hydrotherapy — not forest bathing
- **"Japanese garden"** design studies — related but different from forest-bathing research
- **"Nature-based therapy"** — broader category that includes wilderness therapy, horticulture therapy, animal-assisted therapy, not just shinrin-yoku

## Evidence Markers

- Named protocol: "shinrin-yoku," "forest bathing," "forest therapy," "forest medicine"
- Cross-cultural comparison or non-Japanese sample
- Physiological markers common in Japanese protocol: NK cells, cortisol, blood pressure, HRV, adrenaline/noradrenaline
- References to Li (2010), Miyazaki (2018), Park et al. (2010)
- Population descriptors distinguishing urban vs. rural, East Asian vs. Western

## Escalation Triggers

1. **Studies that look like shinrin-yoku but are labelled differently.** Western researchers often study "nature walks" or "forest therapy" without citing the Japanese literature. The adjudicator must assess protocol similarity: if the study involves guided sensory immersion in a forest for 20–120 minutes with physiological measurement, it is functionally shinrin-yoku regardless of label.

2. **Studies with mixed findings on cross-cultural transfer.** A study showing that Finnish participants respond more strongly than Japanese participants to forest exposure would be highly relevant but confusing for a keyword filter that expects positive transfer evidence. The adjudicator should accept these — divergent findings are the most interesting.

3. **Studies in cultures with animist or spiritual forest traditions (e.g., Amazonian, Scandinavian foraging).** These test generalizability in a different direction — populations with deep cultural connections to forests. Include if physiological or psychological measures are present.

## Unresolved Ambiguities

- Is the question asking about protocol transfer (does the same walking protocol work?) or effect transfer (does forest exposure produce the same effects?). These are different — a protocol may need cultural adaptation (e.g., guided meditation in Japanese vs. silent walking in Western contexts) while still producing equivalent outcomes.
- How different can the forest be? Japanese cedar forests are not the same ecosystem as Pacific Northwest temperate rainforest or English deciduous woodland. Is the question about the social protocol or the ecological setting?

## LLM Drafting Note

This constitution was drafted with LLM panel reasoning. SQ-009 is distinctive because it is a population transfer question rather than a mechanism question. The key intellectual challenge is determining when a Western nature-exposure study is "close enough" to shinrin-yoku to count as a replication attempt. Li and McEwan bridge the Japanese-Western divide on the panel.
