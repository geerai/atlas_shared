# Panel Dossier: SQ-006

## Question

What studies have attempted to empirically separate ART (attention fatigue restoration) from SRT (physiological stress recovery) pathways in the same experimental design?

## Topic / Subtopic

ART vs SRT / Empirical confound

## In-Criteria (Central Cases)

1. The study measures BOTH an attention/cognitive outcome (directed attention fatigue, executive function, sustained attention) AND a stress/physiological outcome (cortisol, HRV, blood pressure, self-reported stress) in the same sample after the same nature exposure, enabling comparison of the two pathways.
2. The study explicitly frames its design around the ART-vs-SRT question, acknowledging the confound and attempting to adjudicate between the two theories.
3. The study uses a mediation or path-analytic framework to test whether the attention benefit of nature exposure is explained by (mediated through) stress reduction, or whether attention restoration and stress recovery are independent pathways.
4. Review or theoretical papers that systematically compare the predictions of ART and SRT and identify where they diverge — these provide the intellectual framework for distinguishing the two theories.

## Out-Criteria (Clear Rejections)

1. Papers testing ART alone (cognitive outcomes only, no stress measure) — these belong to SQ-001.
2. Papers testing SRT alone (physiological stress outcomes only, no cognitive measure) — these belong to SQ-004.
3. Papers that measure multiple outcomes but do not attempt to compare, contrast, or separate the pathways — simply reporting attention and stress as independent outcomes does not address the confound question.
4. Papers about stress and attention in non-environmental contexts (e.g., test anxiety effect on attention) that do not involve nature or environmental manipulation.

## Hard Cases (Borderline Decisions)

1. **Studies measuring both outcomes but not testing mediation**: A paper that reports both attention scores and cortisol after nature walks, but analyzes them as two independent outcomes rather than testing whether one explains the other. *Tend toward: include as partial evidence — the data is there even if the analysis is not. Flag that the mediation test is missing.*

2. **Papers arguing for a unified restorative process**: Some researchers (e.g., Kaplan himself) argue that ART and SRT are not truly separate — attention fatigue is a kind of stress, and restoration is a unitary process. These papers challenge the premise of SQ-006. *Tend toward: include — they are directly relevant, they just answer the question differently (the two pathways are not separable because they are one pathway).*

3. **Nature and rumination studies (Bratman-type)**: Studies showing that nature walks reduce rumination (a form of perseverative negative thinking). Rumination is neither pure attention fatigue nor pure physiological stress — it involves both cognitive and affective processes. *Tend toward: edge case — relevant if the paper discusses whether rumination reduction is an attention process or an affect process.*

4. **Dose-response studies with multiple measures**: Studies varying nature exposure dose (5 min, 20 min, 60 min) and measuring both attention and stress at each dose. These can reveal whether the two outcomes follow different time courses (divergence = separate pathways). *Tend toward: include — time-course divergence is one of the strongest forms of evidence for pathway separation.*

## False Friends

- **"Stress and attention"** in educational or workplace psychology — about academic stress impairing attention, not about nature-based recovery
- **"Cognitive stress"** — often means cognitive load or task difficulty, not stress recovery
- **"Dual-process" theories** — usually refers to System 1/System 2 (Kahneman), not ART vs. SRT
- **"Recovery"** in clinical contexts — rehabilitation, therapy, not environmental restoration

## Evidence Markers

- Dual measurement: attention task + physiological biomarker in same participants
- Mediation analysis, path model, or structural equation model
- Explicit references to both Kaplan (ART) and Ulrich (SRT)
- Phrases: "dual-pathway," "competing mechanisms," "attention vs. stress," "restoration theory comparison"
- Multiple time points allowing time-course comparison

## Escalation Triggers

1. **Papers measuring both attention and stress but never comparing them.** This is the most common case in the literature — researchers report both outcomes in the results section but treat them as independent. The adjudicator must assess whether the paper's design could in principle address the confound, even if the authors did not run the relevant mediation analysis.

2. **Papers that use "restoration" without specifying which type.** The word "restoration" in the environmental psychology literature is deeply ambiguous. The adjudicator must determine from the methods section whether "restoration" is operationalized as attention recovery, stress recovery, or both.

3. **Papers arguing the two theories are fundamentally incomparable.** Some authors argue that ART and SRT make predictions at different levels of analysis (cognitive vs. physiological) and therefore cannot be directly compared. These are directly relevant to SQ-006 even though they challenge the question's premise.

## Unresolved Ambiguities

- Is the ART-vs-SRT separation a genuine empirical question, or is it a philosophical question about levels of explanation? If attention fatigue IS a form of stress response (as some argue), then separating the pathways may be conceptually incoherent, and the question should be reframed.
- How should studies using self-reported stress (not physiological) be treated? Self-reported stress is subjective and may involve cognitive appraisal (conflating it with the attention pathway). Only physiological stress measures offer clean separation.

## LLM Drafting Note

This constitution was drafted with LLM panel reasoning. SQ-006 represents the central confound in the field — the question David's description calls "the central confound." The evidence base is genuinely sparse because very few studies have been designed to separate the pathways. The constitution is therefore deliberately inclusive, accepting papers that provide *any* data on differential pathway dynamics (e.g., different time courses, different dose-response curves, different populations showing one effect but not the other).
