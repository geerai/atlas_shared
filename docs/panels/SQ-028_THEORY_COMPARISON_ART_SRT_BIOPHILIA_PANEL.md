# Panel Dossier: SQ-028

## Question

Are Attention Restoration Theory, Stress Recovery Theory, and the Biophilia Hypothesis three distinct theories or do they converge on the same underlying mechanism?

## Topic / Subtopic

## In-Criteria (Central Cases)

1. The paper explicitly compares, contrasts, or attempts to integrate ART (Kaplan & Kaplan), SRT (Ulrich), and/or the biophilia hypothesis (Wilson/Kellert) as theoretical frameworks.
2. The paper tests predictions derived from two or more of these theories in the same study to determine whether they make distinguishable predictions.
3. The paper proposes a unifying framework, integrated model, or common mechanism that subsumes the three theories.
4. Systematic reviews or meta-analyses that examine whether the evidence base favors one theory over others or whether the theories are empirically interchangeable.

## Out-Criteria (Clear Rejections)

1. Papers testing only one theory without referring to or comparing against the others. These belong to the respective question (SQ-001 for ART, SQ-004 for SRT, SQ-007 for biophilia).
2. Papers about entirely different theoretical traditions (place attachment, environmental identity, ecological psychology) without connecting to ART, SRT, or biophilia.
3. Papers about biophilic design practice that invoke the three theories as background but do not analyze their conceptual relationship.
4. General nature-and-health reviews that cite all three theories as context but treat them as interchangeable labels.

## Hard Cases (Borderline Decisions)

1. **Kaplan (1995) original ART statement**: The foundational theoretical paper. It does not compare to SRT or biophilia, but is essential context. *Tend toward: include as reference context, categorize as ART primary source.*

2. **Processing fluency as alternative**: Joye proposes that processing fluency (ease of visual processing for natural scenes) explains nature preference without the biophilia, ART, or SRT mechanisms. *Tend toward: include — processing fluency is a competing unifying account.*

3. **Evolutionary landscape preference studies**: These inform the biophilia hypothesis and SRT evolutionary claims but may not explicitly discuss ART. *Tend toward: edge case — include if the study discusses theoretical implications across frameworks.*

4. **Pathway models**: Recent frameworks (Markevych et al. 2017) proposing that nature affects health through multiple pathways (restoration, physical activity, social interaction). These integrate rather than compare. *Tend toward: include — pathway frameworks directly address whether the theories are distinct pathways.*

5. **Studies measuring both attention and stress outcomes but not framing it as a theory test**: The data may enable comparison even if the authors did not intend it. *Tend toward: edge case for SQ-006 cross-reference; include here only if theoretical implications are discussed.*

## False Friends

- **"Attention"** studies without ART framework
- **"Stress recovery"** in exercise science — post-exercise physiology
- **"Biophilia"** in conservation ethics — environmental activism
- **"Theory comparison"** in statistics — model comparison, AIC/BIC
- **"Integration"** in neuroscience — multisensory integration

## Evidence Markers

- Explicit naming of at least two theories: ART, SRT, biophilia, Kaplan, Ulrich, Wilson
- Theoretical comparison language: "convergent," "redundant," "complementary," "conflicting predictions"
- Citation patterns: citing both Kaplan and Ulrich in the same paragraph as competing frameworks
- Pathway diagrams, mediation models, or conceptual frameworks
- References to Hartig et al. (2014 review), van den Berg et al. (2007), Joye (2007, 2011), Markevych et al. (2017)

## Escalation Triggers

The following cases MUST be escalated to a subscribed LLM adjudicator rather than resolved by keyword heuristics:

1. **Papers arguing that ART and SRT are not really distinct** — that attention fatigue IS a stress response and "restoration" is a unitary phenomenon. The adjudicator must assess whether the convergence argument is based on empirical evidence (studies measuring both with identical outcomes) or conceptual analysis, and determine whether the claim holds at the mechanistic level.

2. **Papers proposing an entirely new framework** replacing ART, SRT, and biophilia — e.g., processing fluency, predictive coding, embodied cognition as alternative theoretical substrates. The adjudicator must determine whether the new framework generates novel predictions not already covered by ART/SRT/biophilia or merely relabels existing constructs.

3. **Papers from outside environmental psychology** (philosophy of science, theory of scientific reduction) analyzing whether the three theories meet criteria for distinct scientific theories. The adjudicator must assess the philosophical analysis for domain accuracy — does the author understand the empirical evidence? — and flag for theoretical significance regardless of the author's disciplinary background.

## Unresolved Ambiguities

- ART, SRT, and biophilia were developed at different levels of explanation: ART is cognitive, SRT is psychophysiological, biophilia is evolutionary. Comparing them may be a category error.
- Whether the theories are "the same" depends on what counts as "the same mechanism." They may share a common evolutionary origin while operating through distinct proximal mechanisms.
- The field may not have enough data to formally distinguish the theories — they make very similar predictions in most testable scenarios.
