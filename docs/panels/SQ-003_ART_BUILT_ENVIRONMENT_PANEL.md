# Panel Dossier: SQ-003

## Question

Can built indoor environments (offices, classrooms, hospitals) produce attention restoration effects comparable to outdoor nature?

## Topic / Subtopic

Attention Restoration Theory / Built environment

## In-Criteria (Central Cases)

1. The study tests whether an indoor built environment containing nature elements (plants, water features, natural light, nature views, natural materials, nature imagery) produces measurable attention restoration, cognitive recovery, or reduced directed attention fatigue in occupants.
2. The study compares an indoor biophilic condition against a non-biophilic indoor control condition (e.g., office with plants vs. office without plants) using a cognitive or attention-related outcome measure.
3. The study compares indoor nature exposure against outdoor nature exposure to assess whether the indoor condition achieves comparable restoration effects — this directly tests the "transferability" question at the heart of SQ-003.
4. Post-occupancy evaluation (POE) studies of biophilic buildings that include cognitive performance or attention measures alongside satisfaction or wellness outcomes.

## Out-Criteria (Clear Rejections)

1. Papers studying outdoor nature exposure only, with no indoor or built-environment condition — these belong to SQ-001 (core ART evidence), not SQ-003 (built environment transferability).
2. Papers studying indoor environments but measuring only thermal comfort, acoustic satisfaction, or air quality with no psychological or cognitive outcome — these are IEQ studies, not ART studies.
3. Papers about window design, daylighting, or view quality that measure only energy performance, glare, or illuminance without any occupant psychological response — these are building science, not environmental psychology.
4. Papers about biophilic design as an aesthetic or philosophical movement with no empirical data on cognitive or attention outcomes.

## Hard Cases (Borderline Decisions)

1. **Window view studies in hospitals (Ulrich-type)**: Studies showing that patients with nature window views recover faster. These are about indoor occupants benefiting from nature views, but the mechanism may be stress reduction (SRT) rather than attention restoration (ART). *Tend toward: include if any cognitive or attention measure is present; flag as edge case if only clinical outcomes (length of stay, analgesic use) are reported.*

2. **Indoor plants and productivity**: Many studies show that indoor plants improve self-reported productivity, but "productivity" is a fuzzy composite that may or may not involve directed attention. *Tend toward: include if the productivity measure is disaggregated and includes an attention component; edge case if "productivity" is a single self-report item.*

3. **Virtual nature in indoor settings**: Studies using nature videos, nature murals, or VR nature in indoor spaces to test restoration. These are indoor interventions with nature content, but the "nature" is simulated. *Tend toward: include — this is exactly the kind of evidence SQ-003 needs, since the question is whether indoor environments can replicate outdoor effects.*

4. **Green building certification (LEED, WELL) outcome studies**: Studies evaluating occupant outcomes in green-certified buildings. These often include biophilic design elements but the intervention is a bundle (air quality + lighting + biophilia + ...), making it impossible to attribute effects to nature specifically. *Tend toward: edge case — useful background but cannot isolate the biophilic mechanism.*

5. **Classroom daylighting and student performance**: Studies showing that natural daylight in classrooms improves test scores. Daylight is a nature element in indoor design, but the mechanism may be circadian alignment or visual comfort rather than attention restoration. *Tend toward: include if attention or concentration is measured; exclude if only test scores without any process measure.*

## False Friends

- **"Indoor environment quality" (IEQ)** — typically refers to air quality, temperature, humidity, and acoustics, not biophilic design
- **"Built environment and health"** — often means urban planning, walkability, and physical activity, not indoor nature and cognition
- **"Green building"** — refers to energy efficiency and sustainability certification, not necessarily biophilic design
- **"Environmental design"** — may refer to crime prevention through environmental design (CPTED), wayfinding, or other non-biophilic applications

## Evidence Markers

- Named biophilic design elements: indoor plants, living walls, nature views, water features, natural materials, nature imagery, daylight
- Attention or cognitive tasks administered in indoor settings
- Comparison between biophilic and non-biophilic indoor conditions
- References to the 14 Patterns of Biophilic Design (Browning et al., 2014)
- Post-occupancy evaluation (POE) methodology with cognitive outcomes
- References to Kaplan, Ulrich, or ART/SRT frameworks applied to indoor settings

## Escalation Triggers

1. **Papers with "biophilic" in the title but no cognitive outcome.** Many biophilic design papers focus entirely on satisfaction, preference, or aesthetic response. A keyword filter will flag them as relevant, but without a cognitive or attention measure, they do not address SQ-003's specific claim. The adjudicator must determine whether a cognitive outcome is present.

2. **Multi-factor IEQ studies where biophilic elements are one of many variables.** A study measuring the combined effect of air quality, lighting, acoustics, AND indoor plants cannot isolate the biophilic contribution. The adjudicator must assess whether the biophilic element is analyzed separately or only as part of a bundle.

3. **Papers comparing real nature views to simulated nature in indoor settings.** These are highly relevant but the directionality matters — the paper may conclude that simulated nature is inferior, which is still evidence about SQ-003's question.

## Unresolved Ambiguities

- How much "nature" does an indoor environment need to contain before it qualifies as "biophilic" for this question? A single potted plant? A nature photograph? A full living wall? The threshold is poorly defined in the literature.
- Should naturally ventilated buildings (where outdoor air and sounds enter) count as "indoor environments with nature," or are they effectively outdoor? Wind and birdsong entering through open windows blur the indoor/outdoor boundary.

## LLM Drafting Note

This constitution was drafted with LLM panel reasoning. The core challenge of SQ-003 is that it sits at the intersection of ART theory, biophilic design practice, and building performance research — three communities that use different vocabularies and publish in different journals. The panel pairs Seresinhe (computational large-scale evidence) and Pearson (GIS-based indoor/urban health) with Melis (biophilic architecture metrics), Ward Thompson (designed landscapes), and Ulrich (foundational SRT for competing-theory perspective) to ensure cross-disciplinary evidence synthesis grounded in recent empirical methods.
