# Panel Dossier: SQ-021

## Question

How does building spatial complexity and legibility affect wayfinding-related stress and cognitive load in hospital or campus environments?

## Topic / Subtopic

Spatial Complexity / Wayfinding and stress

## In-Criteria (Central Cases)

1. The study measures cognitive load, stress, or wayfinding performance as a function of building spatial complexity or legibility (floor plan configuration, corridor articulation, landmark availability, signage effectiveness).
2. The study is conducted in hospital, campus, airport, or comparably complex public buildings where wayfinding difficulty has practical consequences.
3. The study uses Space Syntax, isovist analysis, or other configurational measures to quantify spatial complexity and relates these to behavioral or psychological wayfinding outcomes.
4. The study tests wayfinding interventions (signage, color coding, landmarks, spatial simplification) and their effect on cognitive load or stress.

## Out-Criteria (Clear Rejections)

1. Papers about outdoor urban wayfinding or GPS navigation without building-interior focus.
2. Papers about signage design as graphic design without wayfinding performance or stress measurement.
3. Papers about spatial complexity in virtual environments (video games) without architectural application.
4. Papers about spatial cognition in cognitive psychology labs using abstract mazes with no building context.

## Hard Cases (Borderline Decisions)

1. **VR wayfinding studies replicating real buildings**: Studies using VR reconstructions of hospitals or campuses. *Tend toward: include — VR allows experimental control impossible in real buildings, and many wayfinding studies use this paradigm.*

2. **Elderly or cognitively impaired populations**: Dementia wayfinding in care facilities. *Tend toward: include — these populations are most vulnerable to spatial complexity and the design implications are critical.*

3. **Space Syntax studies of building configuration without behavioral data**: Purely configurational analyses (integration, connectivity, isovist area) without occupant stress or wayfinding performance data. *Tend toward: edge case — the configuration analysis is necessary but not sufficient without human outcomes.*

4. **Airport or retail wayfinding**: Commercial environments with wayfinding challenges. *Tend toward: include if stress or cognitive load is measured; reject if only footfall or sales metrics are reported.*

5. **Signage studies without spatial complexity measurement**: Testing sign legibility or placement without characterizing the spatial context. *Tend toward: edge case — signage is an intervention, but without spatial complexity data the study doesn't address SQ-021 directly.*

## False Friends

- **"Spatial complexity"** in computational geometry — algorithmic complexity, not building experience
- **"Wayfinding"** in UX design — website navigation
- **"Cognitive load"** in instructional design — learning material difficulty, not spatial navigation
- **"Legibility"** in typography — font readability
- **"Hospital design"** focusing on infection control or clinical workflow without patient/visitor navigation

## Evidence Markers

- Space Syntax metrics: integration, connectivity, intelligibility, axial lines
- Isovist analysis: visible area, occlusivity, compactness
- Wayfinding metrics: time to destination, wrong turns, route efficiency, hesitation count
- Stress measures: self-report anxiety, cortisol, heart rate during navigation
- Cognitive load measures: NASA-TLX, secondary task performance, pupillometry
- References to Hölscher et al. (2006, 2012), Dalton (2003), Passini (1984, 1996), O'Neill (1991)

## Escalation Triggers

The following cases MUST be escalated to a subscribed LLM adjudicator rather than resolved by keyword heuristics:

1. **Studies comparing multiple hospitals or campuses** using the same spatial complexity metrics, enabling cross-building comparison of wayfinding difficulty. The adjudicator must assess whether the comparison uses standardized metrics (Space Syntax integration, isovist area) that allow valid cross-building comparison, and flag these as high-priority evidence.

2. **Studies testing the interaction between spatial complexity and signage** — does signage compensate fully for poor spatial layout, or is there a complexity threshold beyond which signage cannot rescue navigation? The adjudicator must determine whether the study manipulates both variables independently or merely observes their correlation.

3. **Mobile physiological measurement studies** (wearable EEG, wristband HRV) capturing real-time stress during actual wayfinding in buildings. The adjudicator must assess the validity of the ambulatory physiological measures (motion artifacts, baseline control) and determine whether the stress signal can be attributed to wayfinding difficulty rather than physical exertion.

## Unresolved Ambiguities

- "Spatial complexity" can mean floor plan complexity, visual complexity, or decision-point density. These are related but not identical.
- Whether stress is caused by the navigation difficulty itself or by the *consequences* of being lost (embarrassment, being late, missing appointments in hospitals) is rarely disentangled.

## LLM Drafting Note

This constitution was drafted with LLM panel reasoning. SQ-021 bridges spatial cognition research and architectural design evaluation. The main challenge is that Space Syntax and cognitive wayfinding research have developed somewhat independently — Space Syntax focuses on configuration, cognitive science on mental representations. The constitution requires both spatial measurement and human outcomes.
