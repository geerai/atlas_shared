# Panel Dossier: SQ-007

## Question

What experimental evidence supports the biophilia hypothesis — an innate human affinity for natural environments — as opposed to learned preference?

## Topic / Subtopic

Biophilia / Evolutionary basis

## In-Criteria (Central Cases)

1. The study provides experimental or quasi-experimental evidence for an innate (not learned) human preference for natural environments, nature elements, or nature-like patterns — for example, studies of infants or very young children who have minimal cultural exposure to nature.
2. The study tests the universality of nature preference across cultures, especially comparing populations with substantially different nature-exposure histories, to distinguish between innate affinity and culturally acquired preference.
3. The study provides evidence from populations with limited or unusual nature experience (urban-only residents, clinical populations) to test whether nature preference persists even when exposure history is minimal.
4. The study tests specific predictions of the biophilia hypothesis against competing explanations — cultural conditioning, processing fluency, mere exposure effects, or social learning.

## Out-Criteria (Clear Rejections)

1. Papers about the philosophical or ethical dimensions of biophilia (e.g., bioethics of human-animal relationships) without empirical data on nature preference.
2. Papers about biodiversity conservation that invoke "biophilia" as a motivational frame for environmental activism but do not test the psychological hypothesis.
3. Papers about pet ownership, animal-assisted therapy, or human-animal bonding that do not address landscape/environmental preference.
4. Papers about genetic determinism or evolutionary psychology debates that use "innate" as a philosophical concept without testing it empirically.

## Hard Cases (Borderline Decisions)

1. **Nature preference studies in children aged 3-8**: These provide developmental evidence, but by age 3+ children have had substantial cultural exposure. Are these early enough to count as evidence for "innate"? *Tend toward: include — they provide age-curve data that can distinguish maturation from learning, even if not definitive.*

2. **Studies of nature sounds or scents (not landscapes)**: Biophilia may extend beyond visual nature preference to other sensory modalities. A study showing that infants calm to birdsong or flowing water sounds would be relevant, but the biophilia hypothesis is most commonly formulated in visual/landscape terms. *Tend toward: include as extending the hypothesis.*

3. **Twin studies of nature preference**: Studies comparing nature preferences in monozygotic vs. dizygotic twins could provide evidence for genetic vs. environmental origins. These are rare but directly relevant. *Tend toward: include — strong evidence for the innate claim if available.*

4. **Mere exposure studies**: Papers testing whether repeated exposure to nature increases preference (supporting a learning rather than innate mechanism). These are counter-evidence to biophilia and therefore directly relevant. *Tend toward: include — the question asks for evidence "as opposed to learned preference," so counter-evidence belongs.*

5. **Virtual nature studies with no prior real-nature experience**: Studies testing whether individuals who grew up in concrete-heavy urban environments with minimal nature exposure still show nature preference when exposed to virtual nature for the first time. *Tend toward: include as strong evidence one way or the other.*

## False Friends

- **"Biophilia" in biology** — Wilson's original term used in ecology/conservation, not psychology
- **"Biophilic cities"** — urban planning movement, not an empirical test of the hypothesis
- **"Natural selection"** — evolutionary biology, not human-nature affinity
- **"Innate immunity"** — immunology
- **"Environmental learning"** — often about classroom environments, not nature-exposure learning

## Evidence Markers

- Infant or child samples (especially pre-verbal or < 2 years)
- Cross-cultural comparisons (especially isolated or low-nature-exposure populations)
- Preference tasks: forced-choice landscape preferences, looking-time paradigms, implicit association tests
- Age-curve or developmental trajectory data
- References to Wilson (1984), Kellert (1993), Kahn (1997, 1999), Falk & Balling (2010)

## Escalation Triggers

1. **Papers invoking "biophilia" in their title or abstract but studying biodiversity conservation behavior rather than psychological nature preference.** The term is widely used outside psychology. The adjudicator must determine whether the paper tests the psychological hypothesis.

2. **Papers showing cultural variation in nature preference that could be interpreted either way.** Cross-cultural differences might refute universality (supporting learned preference) or reflect different expressions of the same innate drive. The adjudicator must assess how the authors interpret the data.

3. **Papers from evolutionary psychology testing domain-specific cognitive adaptations.** These may provide analogical support for biophilia (e.g., evolved fear of snakes supports evolved threat detection, which is the flip side of evolved safety preference). The adjudicator must decide whether the analogy is close enough.

## Unresolved Ambiguities

- Can "innate" ever be established with certainty? Even infant studies cannot fully exclude prenatal environmental exposure. The panel should treat "innate" as a spectrum (strong genetic component vs. heavily culturally mediated) rather than a binary.
- Should the biophilia hypothesis be tested as a universal claim (all humans prefer nature) or a central-tendency claim (on average, humans prefer nature, but with substantial individual variation)?

## LLM Drafting Note

This constitution was drafted with LLM panel reasoning. SQ-007 is epistemically tricky because the innate-vs-learned distinction is notoriously difficult to test. The constitution deliberately includes counter-evidence (mere exposure, cultural learning studies) because the question explicitly asks about "as opposed to learned preference." LoBue's developmental expertise ensures that infant/child evidence is properly evaluated rather than over-interpreted.
